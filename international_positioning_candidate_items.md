# International Positioning & World Order — Candidate Scale Items

## Board Overview

Three dimensions capturing how a leader/governance philosophy positions the polity relative to the external world: sovereignty prioritization, cooperation-competition orientation, and stance on international order and supranational responsibility.

All items are phrased as first-person governance philosophy statements ("I believe…" / "A good ruler…" / "The state should…") to work for both self-report and simulated historical-leader response.

Response format assumed: 7-point Likert (1 = Strongly Disagree … 7 = Strongly Agree), with reverse-scored items noted [R].

---

## Dimension A: 主权与自主性观 (Sovereignty & Autonomy)

**Polarity: UNIPOLAR** — higher scores = stronger sovereignty-first orientation.

| # | Candidate Item | Source Scale / Subscale | Rationale | Suggested Sub-construct |
|---|---------------|------------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| A1 | "The most important duty of a ruler is to preserve the independence and self-determination of the state." | Adapted from Kosterman & Feshbach (1989) Patriotism subscale ("I love my country"; "It is important to me to serve my country") | Core patriotic attachment rephrased as governance obligation; captures baseline sovereignty salience without chauvinism. | **Core sovereignty salience** |
| A2 | "No external power, institution, or alliance should have the authority to override decisions made by the legitimate government of a state." | Adapted from Internationalism Scale (Kosterman & Feshbach, 1989) — reverse-coded internationalism items; also informed by Druckman (1994) National Attachment "exclusive" items | Directly measures rejection of supranational override; historically applicable from Westphalian sovereignty through anti-colonial movements. | **External constraint rejection** |
| A3 | "A state that cannot sustain itself without depending on foreign aid or foreign protection has failed in its primary purpose." | Adapted from Schatz, Staub & Lavine (1999) "Blind Patriotism" scale ("My country, right or wrong") combined with Collective Narcissism items on group self-sufficiency | Captures autarky/self-reliance as governance value; applicable to mercantilist, revolutionary, and nationalist leaders. | **Self-reliance / autarky** |
| A4 | "International agreements are only legitimate to the extent that they can be revoked when they no longer serve the state's interests." | Adapted from Hurwitz & Peffley (1987) foreign policy attitude structure; informed by Wittkopf (1990) Militant Internationalism items on unilateral action | Measures instrumental view of international commitments; captures sovereignty-preserving conditionality. | **Conditional commitment to international obligations** |
| A5 | "The cultural traditions, governing institutions, and way of life of my polity deserve special protection from foreign influence." | Adapted from Kosterman & Feshbach (1989) Nationalism subscale ("The United States is the best country"); Collective Narcissism Scale (Golec de Zavala et al., 2009) items on group uniqueness | Captures civilizational-protective sovereignty without requiring modern nation-state framing; works for imperial, dynastic, and nation-state contexts. | **Cultural-civilizational sovereignty** |
| A6 | "When the interests of my state conflict with the wishes of the international community, my state's interests must come first." | Adapted from Kosterman & Feshbach (1989) Nationalism subscale; Blank & Schmidt (2003) National Identity items on national superiority | Direct sovereignty-vs-internationalism trade-off item; the strongest "sovereignty-first" statement in the set. | **Sovereignty primacy in conflict** |
| A7 | "A wise ruler guards against entangling alliances and commitments that limit the state's freedom of action." | Informed by VICS/Operational Code P-4 indicator (propensity for risk) and classical realist doctrine; adapted language from Holsti (1970) operational code analysis | Historical-register item evoking Washington's Farewell Address, Chinese non-alignment, etc.; tests strategic autonomy preference. | **Strategic autonomy / non-entanglement** |
| A8 | "Allowing foreign judges or foreign laws to govern any aspect of domestic affairs is an unacceptable surrender of sovereignty." | Adapted from Internationalism Scale (reverse); informed by Bizumic & Duckitt (2012) ethnocentrism-sovereignty items | Targets legal/jurisdictional sovereignty specifically; highly relevant to ICC, EU law, extraterritorial jurisdiction debates across eras. | **Legal-jurisdictional sovereignty** |
| A9 | "The state's right to control who enters and leaves its territory is a non-negotiable foundation of governance." | Adapted from Blank & Schmidt (2003) nationalism items; informed by Collective Narcissism boundary-protection themes | Captures territorial sovereignty through border control—a concrete, cross-era governance concern from walled cities to modern immigration. | **Territorial integrity / border sovereignty** |

**Dimension A Total: 9 items**

## Dimension B: 对外合作—竞争取向 (External Cooperation vs. Competition Orientation)

**Polarity: BIPOLAR** — high scores = cooperation orientation; low scores = competition/zero-sum orientation.

Items labeled [COOP] are scored as-is (agreement = cooperation pole). Items labeled [COMP] are reverse-scored (agreement = competition pole).

| # | Candidate Item | Source Scale / Subscale | Rationale | Suggested Sub-construct |
|---|---------------|------------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| B1 | "Relations between states are best understood as opportunities for mutual benefit rather than contests for dominance." [COOP] | Adapted from Wittkopf (1990) Cooperative Internationalism Scale ("The US should cooperate more with the UN"); Holsti & Rosenau (1990) cooperative internationalism factor | Core framing item: do you see the external world as positive-sum or zero-sum? Directly maps onto VICS P-1 (nature of the political universe). | **Positive-sum vs. zero-sum worldview** |
| B2 | "In dealings with other states, one must always assume that they will pursue their own advantage at your expense unless restrained." [COMP] | Adapted from Wittkopf (1990) Militant Internationalism Scale; informed by VICS P-1 (hostile vs. friendly) and Hurwitz & Peffley (1987) "threat" items | Captures Hobbesian/realist assumption about other actors' intent; reverse-scored to place on cooperation end. | **Threat assumption / defensive realism** |
| B3 | "Shared prosperity between states strengthens everyone; what benefits my neighbor ultimately benefits me." [COOP] | Adapted from Cooperative Internationalism items on foreign aid and trade; Reysen & Katzarska-Miller (2013) Global Citizenship Scale intergroup empathy items | Tests economic positive-sum belief; works across mercantilist ("trade enriches") and liberal internationalist frames. | **Economic positive-sum belief** |
| B4 | "A state that grows more powerful inevitably becomes a threat to its neighbors." [COMP] | Adapted from VICS P-2 (realization of political values — optimistic vs. pessimistic); offensive realism framing per Mearsheimer | Captures structural competition/power-transition anxiety; reverse-scored. Applicable from Thucydides to modern geopolitics. | **Power as threat / structural competition** |
| B5 | "The best way to ensure peace is to build strong, binding partnerships and institutions with other states." [COOP] | Adapted from Cooperative Internationalism Scale (multilateral security items); Wittkopf (1990) | Tests institutional-cooperative security belief; distinguishes from pure balance-of-power thinking. | **Institutional cooperation for security** |
| B6 | "Negotiation and compromise are more effective tools of statecraft than the threat or use of force." [COOP] | Adapted from VICS I-1 (strategic approach to goals: cooperative vs. conflictual) and Wittkopf (1990) MI items (reverse) | Directly parallels VICS operational code distinction between cooperative and conflictual strategic orientations. | **Diplomatic vs. coercive preference** |
| B7 | "In a world of competing states, the gains of one side usually come at the expense of the other." [COMP] | Adapted from Militant Internationalism zero-sum items; Social Dominance Orientation (Pratto et al., 1994) competitive worldview items | Pure zero-sum belief statement; reverse-scored. Cross-culturally and historically intelligible. | **Zero-sum / relative gains thinking** |
| B8 | "Opening the state's markets and borders to exchange with others creates more wealth than protecting them." [COOP] | Adapted from Cooperative Internationalism trade items; informed by economic liberalism vs. mercantilism distinction | Economic cooperation operationalized through trade openness; a concrete policy-adjacent item balancing the abstract B1/B3. | **Openness vs. protectionism** |
| B9 | "A ruler must be prepared to use deception and force against foreign adversaries; trust between states is a dangerous illusion." [COMP] | Adapted from VICS I-1 (conflictual strategic orientation) and Machiavellian statecraft framing; informed by Militant Internationalism | Captures the extreme competition pole with Machiavellian/realist flavor; works across all historical periods. | **Strategic distrust / Machiavellian realism** |
| B10 | "When conflicts arise between states, a solution that partially satisfies all parties is usually preferable to a total victory for one side." [COOP] | Adapted from VICS I-5 (utility of means: compromise/settlement); Cooperative Internationalism diplomatic items | Tests preference for compromise over domination; key discriminator between cooperative and competitive orientations. | **Compromise preference vs. victory maximization** |

**Dimension B Total: 10 items (5 COOP, 5 COMP for balance)**

## Dimension C: 国际秩序立场与外部责任观 (International Order Position & External Responsibility)

**Polarity: COMPLEX** — combines (C-i) perception of one's role/position in the international order with (C-ii) willingness to bear supranational responsibility. Items are organized by sub-construct.

| # | Candidate Item | Source Scale / Subscale | Rationale | Suggested Sub-construct |
|---|---------------|------------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| C1 | "My state has a responsibility to contribute to the well-being of peoples beyond its borders, even when doing so yields no direct benefit." [C-ii] | Adapted from Morais & Ogden (2011) Global Social Responsibility Scale ("I think that people in developed nations have an obligation to help…"); Reysen & Katzarska-Miller (2013) Global Citizenship Scale responsibility subscale | Core altruistic-responsibility item; captures supranational duty independent of self-interest. | **Altruistic supranational duty** |
| C2 | "A great state should seek to shape the rules and institutions that govern relations among all states." [C-i] | Adapted from Internationalism Scale (Kosterman & Feshbach, 1989) items on international engagement; informed by Holsti (1970) operational code "role conception" | Captures order-shaping ambition; works for both hegemonic and reformist leaders. Neutral on whether the order is liberal, imperial, or revolutionary. | **Order-shaping ambition** |
| C3 | "The suffering of people in other lands is as morally compelling as the suffering of my own people." [C-ii] | Adapted from Reysen & Katzarska-Miller (2013) Global Citizenship Scale intergroup empathy items; Cosmopolitan Orientation Scale (Leung et al., 2015) universal concern items | Tests moral universalism—the philosophical foundation of external responsibility. Cross-cultural resonance (Buddhist compassion, Confucian ren, Christian charity, Islamic ummah). | **Moral universalism / cosmopolitan empathy** |
| C4 | "My state should be willing to bear costs—military, economic, or political—to uphold principles of justice in the wider world." [C-ii] | Adapted from Morais & Ogden (2011) Global Social Responsibility "willingness to act" items; Wittkopf (1990) Cooperative Internationalism items on foreign aid burden | Measures cost-bearing willingness; transforms abstract responsibility into concrete governance commitment. | **Cost-bearing for global justice** |
| C5 | "The existing international order largely reflects the interests and values of my state or states like mine." [C-i] | Original item informed by Operational Code P-2 (optimism about realization of values within existing order); post-colonial critique literature | Captures perceived alignment with current order—a crucial moderator of whether a leader seeks to maintain or revise the system. | **Status-quo alignment / order fit** |
| C6 | "It is legitimate for powerful states to intervene in the affairs of others to prevent grave injustice, even without invitation." [C-i + C-ii] | Adapted from Cooperative Internationalism humanitarian intervention items; informed by R2P (Responsibility to Protect) discourse | Tests the strongest form of external responsibility—coercive cosmopolitanism. Highly discriminating; many sovereignty-prioritizers and many cosmopolitans will diverge here. | **Interventionist responsibility / R2P** |
| C7 | "My state should lead by the example of its own governance rather than by imposing its model on others." [C-i] | Adapted from Internationalism Scale; informed by non-interference doctrines (Chinese Five Principles, ASEAN Way); contrasts with C6 | Captures "lighthouse" vs. "crusader" role conception; balances the Western interventionist skew of many scales. | **Exemplar vs. crusader role** |
| C8 | "The international order should be reformed to give a greater voice to those who have been historically marginalized." [C-i] | Original item informed by revisionist-state literature (Schweller, 1994); Global South discourse; Operational Code role of "revolutionary" state | Captures revisionist/reformist orientation toward order; essential for non-Western and revolutionary leaders who reject the status quo but may still accept supranational frameworks. | **Order revision / justice-seeking** |
| C9 | "Future generations everywhere, not only in my own state, have a claim on the decisions I make today." [C-ii] | Adapted from Global Social Responsibility Scale intergenerational items; Cosmopolitan Orientation Scale temporal-cosmopolitan items | Captures temporal + spatial expansion of responsibility—obligations extending beyond current citizens and beyond borders simultaneously. | **Intergenerational global stewardship** |
| C10 | "A state that pursues only its own interests while ignoring the common good of the world ultimately undermines itself." [C-ii] | Adapted from Internationalism Scale; informed by Reysen & Katzarska-Miller (2013) interconnectedness items and Keohane (1984) institutional interdependence arguments | Tests enlightened self-interest as a bridge between sovereignty and cosmopolitan responsibility; allows realists and idealists to agree for different reasons. | **Enlightened self-interest / interdependence** |

**Dimension C Total: 10 items**

## Gap Analysis

### 1. Source Scale Biases

| Gap | Description | Recommendation |
|-----|-------------|----------------|
| **Western liberal-internationalist skew** | Most reference scales (Wittkopf, Kosterman & Feshbach, Reysen) were developed and validated on US/Western European samples. The "desirable" pole implicitly defaults to liberal internationalism. | Items A7, B2, B4, B9, C5, C7, C8 were specifically drafted to give adequate voice to sovereignty-first, realist, and Global South perspectives. Validate with non-Western coders and pilot in cross-cultural samples. |
| **Citizen-attitude framing** | Original scales measure citizen attitudes toward foreign policy ("The US should…"), not governance philosophy. Direct transplantation risks measuring policy preference rather than philosophical orientation. | All items have been reframed as first-person governance principles. Cognitive interviews with historians role-playing leaders (e.g., Bismarck, Nehru, Mao) should confirm role-appropriateness. |
| **Modern nation-state assumption** | Scales assume a Westphalian nation-state context. Many items break down for pre-modern empires, city-states, or non-state political entities. | Language uses "state" or "polity" rather than "nation" or "country." Items avoid references to specific modern institutions (UN, ICC) in the stem, though these can appear in supplementary anchoring prompts. |

### 2. Construct Coverage Gaps

| Gap | Missing Construct | Dimension Affected | Recommendation |
|-----|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Civilizational mission / mandate of heaven** | No existing Western scale captures the idea that a polity has a transcendent civilizational mission (tianxia, Roman imperium, caliphate). This differs from both sovereignty and cosmopolitanism. | Primarily C (order-shaping), partly A (civilizational sovereignty) | Draft 2–3 additional items in revision round, e.g., "My polity carries a civilizational mission that gives it a special role in the world." Source: no existing validated scale—new construct needed. |
| **Revolutionary order-rejection** | Existing scales assume one operates within or toward an international order. Leaders who reject the very legitimacy of the current order (Mao, Khomeini, early Soviet leadership) are poorly captured. | C (order position) | Item C8 partially covers this. Consider adding: "The existing international order is fundamentally unjust and must be replaced, not reformed." |
| **Imperial universalism vs. cosmopolitan universalism** | The difference between "the world should be unified under my state's leadership" (imperial universalism) and "all states should cooperate as equals" (cosmopolitan universalism) is under-differentiated. | C (C2 is ambiguous by design, but may need splitting) | Consider splitting C2 into two items: one capturing "shape rules as an equal participant" and another capturing "lead or dominate the system." |
| **Economic sovereignty / development model** | The economic dimension of sovereignty (industrial policy, monetary independence, control over resources) is only touched indirectly in A3 and B8. | A + B intersection | Draft: "A state must control its own essential industries and resources rather than depend on foreign markets." |
| **Diaspora / transnational identity** | For empires, religious polities, and diaspora nations, the "inside/outside" boundary assumed by sovereignty items is porous. | A (boundary definition) | Consider: "The bonds of shared identity extend beyond the borders of any single state." This would moderate sovereignty scores for diaspora/religious-empire leaders. |
| **Relative vs. absolute gains** | A key theoretical distinction in IR (Grieco 1988 vs. Keohane 1984) only partially captured by B7. | B | B7 covers this conceptually but a second item could sharpen: "In negotiations with other states, I am more concerned with whether my state gains more than the other side than with the absolute size of the gain." |

### 3. Psychometric Concerns

| Issue | Description | Recommendation |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Social desirability** | Cooperation-pole items in Dim B may be more socially desirable in most contemporary contexts, creating acquiescence bias. | Ensure equal numbers of cooperation-keyed and competition-keyed items (done: 5+5). Consider forced-choice pairs in final instrument. |
| **Historical anachronism risk** | Some items (C6 on intervention, C9 on intergenerational stewardship) may be anachronistic for pre-modern leaders. | Provide era-appropriate anchoring prompts in the simulated-leader protocol. Consider marking items as "modern-only" vs. "universal." |
| **Dimensional bleed** | A6 (sovereignty vs. international community) overlaps with B1 (cooperation vs. competition framing); C2 (order-shaping) could load on A (sovereignty) for imperial leaders. | Plan confirmatory factor analysis with oblique rotation. Accept moderate inter-dimensional correlations (expected r ≈ .3–.5 between A and B-reverse). |
| **Response compression for extreme leaders** | Maximally sovereignty-oriented leaders (isolationists, totalitarians) may ceiling on Dim A and floor on Dim B, producing low variance. | Include at least 2 items per dimension at moderate difficulty (A4, A7 for Dim A; B6, B10 for Dim B; C7, C10 for Dim C) to spread responses. |

### 4. Missing Reference Scale Content

| Scale Content Not Yet Represented | Original Scale | Why It Was Excluded | Reconsider? |
|-----------------------------------|---------------|---------------------|-------------|
| "I feel proud to be [nationality]" | Kosterman & Feshbach Patriotism | Emotion-of-pride is citizen attitude, not governance philosophy | No—covered implicitly by A1 and A5 |
| "The US should take military action to prevent aggression" | Wittkopf Militant Internationalism | Too policy-specific and US-centric | Partially captured in B9 and C6 in universal form |
| "I feel a strong sense of belonging to the world community" | Reysen & Katzarska-Miller Global Citizenship | Identity-feeling rather than governance principle | Partially captured in C3 and C10; consider adding a belonging item for Dim C |
| Collective Narcissism items on "recognition" and "special treatment" | Golec de Zavala et al. (2009) | Narcissistic demand for recognition is a psychological dynamic, not a governance philosophy per se | Could add to Dim A as optional "narcissistic sovereignty" marker: "Other states do not give my polity the recognition and respect it deserves." |
